# 2. Core Sales Philosophy

## 2.1 Diagnose Before Pitching

Do not pitch the system too early.

The strongest sale happens when the prospect first sees the problem clearly.

The sequence should be:

1. Understand the business
2. Map the current enquiry process
3. Identify leaks
4. Quantify the impact
5. Show the missing system
6. Recommend the right package
7. Handle concerns
8. Close next step

Do not begin with:

> We offer AI, ads, CRM, and automation.

Begin with:

> Let’s look at what happens when someone contacts your business and where opportunities might be slipping through.

---

## 2.2 Sell The Problem Before The Product

The prospect must understand that the problem is not just “not enough leads.”

The real problem may be:

- Missed calls
- Slow replies
- Weak enquiry capture
- No follow-up
- Quotes not being chased
- Old leads forgotten
- No pipeline
- No source tracking
- Owner manually handling everything

Once the prospect understands that, the system becomes the logical solution.

---

## 2.3 Sell Outcomes, Not Features

Do not sell:

- CRM
- Automations
- AI voice
- Ad manager
- Workflows
- Forms
- Pipelines

Sell:

- More booked jobs
- Less chasing
- Fewer missed opportunities
- Faster replies
- Better follow-up
- More control
- Less admin
- Cleaner job pipeline

Feature language should only be used after the buyer understands the outcome.

---

## 2.4 Stay Practical And Honest

Do not overpromise.

Do not guarantee exact job numbers.

Do not say the system will fix bad service, bad pricing, no demand, or poor reputation.

Use grounded language:

> This system improves the process around the enquiry. It helps capture, respond, follow up, and organise opportunities better.

---

## 2.5 Position Against The Wrong Alternatives

The prospect may compare the offer to:

- Lead sellers
- Ad agencies
- CRM software
- Receptionists
- Doing nothing
- Doing it themselves

Reframe the comparison.

The system is not one of those things.

It is:

> A managed front-desk system that handles the enquiry process from first contact to booked quote.

# 5. Lead Qualification

Not every prospect is a good fit.

## 5.1 Good-Fit Signals

A good-fit prospect usually has:

- Trade or home service business
- Receives calls or quote requests
- High enough job value
- Wants more jobs or better systems
- Misses calls or struggles with follow-up
- Has capacity or wants better-quality jobs
- Can afford setup and monthly fee
- Understands that follow-up matters
- Wants help, not just cheap leads

## 5.2 Bad-Fit Signals

A bad-fit prospect may:

- Want guaranteed jobs only
- Refuse to provide information
- Have no budget
- Have no capacity
- Have low-value jobs only
- Refuse to respond to enquiries
- Want pay-after-results only
- Blame everyone else
- Not care about customer service
- Want a magic solution
- Want no setup fee or monthly fee

## 5.3 Qualification Questions

Ask early:

- What trade or service do you provide?
- How many enquiries do you get in a normal week?
- What is an average good job worth?
- Are you looking for more enquiries, better follow-up, or both?
- Who currently handles calls and enquiries?
- Do you have capacity to take more work?
- Are you currently using any CRM or job management system?
- Have you tried ads or lead providers before?
- Are you looking for a done-for-you setup or something you manage yourself?

The goal of the audit call is to diagnose the enquiry process and recommend the right system.

The call should reveal:

- Where enquiries come from
- What happens when they arrive

- What should be fixed first

> A lot of trade businesses think they need more leads first, but often the bigger issue is what happens after the enquiry comes in. If calls are missed, forms sit in email, or quotes are not followed up, more leads can just create more chaos. So we look at the front-desk process first.

# 9. Current Enquiry Process Questions

## 9.1 Main Enquiry Sources

Ask:

- Where do most enquiries come from right now?
- Phone calls?
- Website?
- Google Business Profile?
- Facebook or Instagram?
- Referrals?
- Email?
- Paid ads?
- Old customers?
- Do you know which source brings the best jobs?

## 9.2 Process Mapping Question

Use this core question:

> Walk me through what happens when someone wants a quote from you.

Then ask follow-ups:

- Who gets the enquiry?
- How fast do they reply?
- Where is it recorded?
- What happens if the customer does not reply?
- What happens if the quote is sent?
- Who follows it up?

## 9.3 Listen For Problems

Listen for:

- “I usually call them back later.”
- “It comes through to my email.”
- “I try to remember.”
- “My wife/admin handles some of it.”
- “We use texts mostly.”
- “I do not really track it.”
- “If they do not reply, we leave it.”
- “Quotes are followed up when we get time.”

These phrases indicate leaks.

# 10. Missed Call Questions

## 10.1 Ask

- How often do you miss calls during the day?
- What happens when you miss a call?
- Do they get an automatic text back?
- Do you call every missed call back?
- How quickly?
- Do you know if missed callers call someone else?
- Are missed calls logged anywhere?
- Do urgent calls get handled differently?

## 10.2 Problem Reframe

If they miss calls:

> That is common for tradies because you are on-site and cannot always answer. The issue is not missing the call. The issue is whether the customer gets a response before they move on to the next business.

## 10.3 Pain Question

> If one missed call was a good job, what could that be worth roughly?

## 10.4 System Link

> This is where the Missed Call Saver comes in. If you miss the call, the system texts the customer instantly and asks what they need help with so the enquiry does not go cold.

---

# 11. Website And Form Questions

## 11.1 Ask

- Do you have a quote form on the website?
- What happens after someone fills it in?
- Does it go to email only?
- Does the customer get an instant response?
- Does it collect job location?
- Does it collect photos?
- Does it ask what service they need?
- Does it ask urgency?
- Is it connected to a pipeline or just an email?
- Who follows it up?

## 11.2 Problem Reframe

> A website form is only useful if something happens after it is submitted. If it just sends an email and waits for you to reply manually, good enquiries can still go cold.

## 11.3 System Link

> The system connects quote requests to an instant response, follow-up, owner notification, and pipeline so the enquiry is not just sitting in an inbox.

---

# 12. Follow-Up Questions

## 12.1 Ask

- What happens if a new enquiry does not reply?
- Do you follow up manually?
- How many times?
- What happens after a quote is sent?
- Do you have a set quote follow-up process?
- Do you ever forget to follow up?
- Do you feel awkward chasing quotes?
- Do you recontact old leads?
- Do you have a list of old quotes?

## 12.2 Problem Reframe

> A lot of jobs are not lost because the customer said no. They are lost because no one followed up properly after the first conversation or quote.

## 12.3 Pain Question

> How many quotes would you say go out each month without a proper follow-up process?

## 12.4 System Link

> This is where the Follow-Up Engine and Quote Follow-Up system help. They make sure customers get polite reminders without you needing to remember every one manually.

---

# 13. Pipeline And Tracking Questions

## 13.1 Ask

- Where do you track open enquiries?
- Is it in your phone, email, notebook, spreadsheet, CRM, or job system?
- Can you see which enquiries need a callback today?
- Can you see which quotes need follow-up?
- Do you track won and lost opportunities?
- Do you know where your best jobs come from?
- Do you have a list of past enquiries?

## 13.2 Problem Reframe

> If opportunities are spread across texts, emails, missed calls, and memory, the business becomes harder to manage as it grows.

## 13.3 System Link

> The simple job pipeline gives you one place to see what is new, what needs a call, what has been quoted, and what needs follow-up.

---

# 14. Value And Cost Questions

These questions help quantify the problem.

## 14.1 Ask

- What is an average good job worth?
- What is a high-value job worth?
- How many enquiries do you get per week?
- How many calls do you miss per week?
- How many quotes do you send per month?
- How many quotes go unfollowed?
- If the system helped recover one or two extra good jobs per month, would that be worthwhile?
- What would it cost you to hire someone to manage calls and follow-up?

## 14.2 Value Reframe

> The system does not need to create hundreds of jobs to make sense. If it helps recover a few good opportunities that would otherwise be missed or forgotten, it can be valuable.

# 15. Diagnosis Script

After discovery, summarise the issues clearly.

## 15.1 Diagnosis Format

Use this structure:

> From what you have told me, there are three main things happening.
>
> First, {{problem_1}}.
>
> Second, {{problem_2}}.
>
> Third, {{problem_3}}.
>
> The good news is that these are system problems, not trade-quality problems. Your business does the work, but the enquiry process needs a better front-desk system.

---

## 15.2 Example Diagnosis: Missed Calls And Follow-Up

> From what you have told me, the biggest leaks are missed calls during the day, website enquiries going to email without instant follow-up, and quotes not being followed up consistently. That means you may already be getting opportunities, but some of them are going cold before they become booked jobs.

---

## 15.3 Example Diagnosis: Already Busy

> You are not necessarily short of work right now. The issue is that because you are busy, the enquiry process depends heavily on you remembering who to call, who to follow up, and which quotes are still open. That is where a front-desk system can help.

---

## 15.4 Example Diagnosis: Growth Needed

> It sounds like there are two issues. First, the current enquiry process needs to be tightened up so calls, forms, and follow-ups are handled properly. Second, once that is in place, you could benefit from more enquiry volume through campaigns or old lead reactivation.

---

# 19. Pricing Conversation

## 19.1 Pricing Principles

When presenting price:

- Be calm
- Do not apologise
- Do not rush
- Tie price to value
- Separate setup and monthly
- Explain what each covers
- Anchor against lost jobs and admin cost
- Recommend the right package, not always the highest

---

## 19.2 Setup Fee Explanation

> The setup fee covers building the system around your business: services, locations, forms, messages, pipeline, missed-call flow, follow-up, owner notifications, testing, and launch. This is not just turning on software. It is a custom front-desk setup for how your business handles enquiries.

---

## 19.3 Monthly Fee Explanation

> The monthly fee keeps the system live, managed, monitored, and improved. It covers the platform, support, message updates, workflow checks, reporting, and ongoing management so you are not left alone with another piece of software.

---

## 19.4 Price Anchor Script

> The way to think about this is not as another software cost. Compare it to the value of missed calls, forgotten quote follow-ups, and hiring someone to manage admin. If one good job is worth a few thousand dollars, the system does not need to recover many opportunities to make sense.

---

The setup builds the system, and the monthly keeps it live, managed, and improved. # 27. Sales Roleplay Examples

## 27.1 Example: Busy Roofer

Prospect:

> We get calls, but I miss a lot during the day.

Response:

> That is normal in roofing because you are not sitting at a desk. The issue is not that you miss the call. The issue is whether that missed caller gets a response before they call the next roofer. The first thing I would install is missed-call text-back and a simple follow-up process.

---

## 27.2 Example: Electrician With Enough Work

Prospect:

> We are already busy. I do not need more leads.

Response:

> That makes sense. In that case, I would not pitch you more ads. I would look at whether the enquiries you already get are being handled properly. Busy businesses often lose opportunities because follow-up becomes inconsistent when the workload is high.

---

## 27.3 Example: Plumber Burned By Ads

Prospect:

> We tried ads before and they were rubbish.

Response:

> That is common. The issue may have been the ads, but often it is also the system after the lead comes in. If customers are not replied to fast enough or followed up properly, the spend gets wasted. That is why I would fix the front-desk process first before putting more money into campaigns.

---

## 27.4 Example: Landscaper Worried About AI

Prospect:

> I do not want AI talking to my customers.

Response:

> Fair enough. We do not need to start with AI voice. The first version can simply text missed callers, reply to quote forms, collect details, and notify you. AI phone support is only an option if it suits the business.

# 29. Master Sales Summary

The sales process for The Tradie Front Desk System should feel like a practical diagnosis, not a marketing pitch.

The strongest sales sequence is:

1. Audit the enquiry process
2. Identify the leaks
3. Quantify the missed opportunity
4. Explain the missing front-desk system
5. Recommend the right package
6. Show how it reduces missed calls, slow replies, and manual follow-up
7. Close with a clear next step

The main message is:

# **More Booked Jobs. Less Chasing. No Tech Headaches.**

The main sales reframe is:

> Before you spend more money getting enquiries, make sure your business can capture, respond to, and follow up with the enquiries it already receives.

The main offer explanation is:

> We give your trade business a smart front desk that catches enquiries, replies fast, follows up, and keeps job opportunities organised.

The main close is:

> Based on the audit, the leaks are clear. The next step is to install the front-desk system so those opportunities are handled consistently instead of manually.
